Island Sustainability

A paradox exists on the Galapagos Islands. Hundreds of thousands of tourists come to the islands annually, hoping to see Darwin's land. In their quest for a glimpse of the islands' iconic species and a sense of unique isolation, tourists increase the human impact on the islands by driving development. Tourists' very presence changes the islands into something other than what attracts visitors in the first place. As the tourism industry grows, it propels the expansion of the local population. The resilience of the islands, their capacity to meet human demands and the islands' future all remain unclear. The sustainability of the islands' identity in the midst of its current situation is questionable.

Social & Ecological Change

With an increase in population has come social and ecological change in the Galapagos Islands. Rural to urban migration occurs more frequently as economic activity grows in urban areas. Although 97 percent of the Galapagos is set aside as the Galapagos National Park, land swapping has occurred recently between National Park plots and areas allotted for human use. Tourism-driven development has resulted in large amounts of construction. Invasive species have come to the islands, both via tourists themselves and on cargo ships bringing supplies to the island. Invasive species compete with native species for resources, threatening the biodiversity of the islands. The full impact of increased development on the environment remains unknown.

Fragile Environments

Because the Galapagos Islands are volcanic, they are missing many of the geological features that support human habitation. Instead of fresh groundwater, the islands have freshwater lagoons protected from the sea by narrow stretches of sand or mangrove forest. Construction of docks and other manmade structures, along with pollution, have threatened this fragile water system and the plants and animals that call mangroves and lagoons home. Grasses and other plants from the highlands of the Galapagos are threatened by invasive species that have adapted in more competitive environments. These plants, such as guava and raspberry, can replace or outshade native ferns and mosses, which are some of the most integral species in highland ecosystems.

Biodiversity

The Galapagos Islands boasts some of the highest numbers of endemic species, or plants and animals found only on the Galapagos. The islands receive nearly 200,000 annual visitors, who come mainly for the islands' isolated environment and the its quirky, unique plants and animals. Galapagos biodiversity is considered an unbalanced ecosystem in terms of species diversity due to its remoteness. Island species were able to adapt and thrive in a strange environment, at times bereft of predators or other challenges found in continental ecosystems.
With a change in global climate, El Niño events will become more intense, thus further affecting plant and animals species dependent on sea level and temperature.

The Human Dimension

A local population inhabits four of the Galapagos Islands, although the Galapagos has no indigenous population. Fishing, agriculture and tourism drive the economy. The expanding tourism industry attracts many of the islands' residents, leading to changes in occupation. It also draws in migrants from mainland Ecuador and other countries. In response, a law passed in 1998 limits and regulates migration. Infrastructure has not kept up with the increasing human demand, leading to a heavy dependence on imported resources, poor water and electrical infrastructure, and social issues such as health care and education. Limited licenses in the tourism and fishing sectors have led to illegal activity and overfishing.
